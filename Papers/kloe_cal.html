Abstract

The KLOE detector was designed primarily for the study of CP violation in neutral kaon decays at DAΦNE, the Frascati φ-factory. The detector consists of a tracker and an electromagnetic calorimeter. A lead-scintillating-fiber sampling calorimeter satisfies best the requirements of the experiment, providing adequate energy resolution and superior timing accuracy. We describe in the following the construction of the calorimeter, its calibration and how the calorimeter information is used to obtain energy, point of entry and time of the arrival of photons, electrons and charged particles. With e+e− collision data at DAΦNE for an integrated luminosity of some we find for electromagnetic showers, an energy resolution of and a time resolution of ps. We also present a measurement of efficiency for low energy photons.

1. Introduction

The KLOE detector [1] and [2], primarily designed for the study of CP violation in the decays of neutral kaons, [1] has begun taking data at the Frascati φ-factory [3]. The experiment was designed to achieve an accuracy for the parameter of . In order to achieve such final precision, the systematic uncertainty in the measurement of the branching ratios for KL,S→π+π− and π0π0 must be kept below . This in turn requires that each contribution to the measurement uncertainty be kept well below 1×10−4. The electromagnetic calorimeter and a large drift chamber are the two main components of the detector, see Fig. 1. The detector dimensions are driven by the mean decay length of KL-mesons which at DAΦNE is . The detector dimensions indicated in Fig. 1 result in ∼30% of the KL-mesons decaying in a usable fiducial volume. Chamber and calorimeter are inside a super conductor coil which produces a field of . The primary functions of the calorimeter are the measurement of photon energies and position of entry. It also provides an accurate measurement of the time of arrival of particles.

Photons occur copiously in the decay of neutral pions, for example from KSKL→nπ0's as well as from many other reactions in φ-decays. The determination of the distance that KL-mesons have travelled before decaying to π0's is of crucial importance in the study of CP violation. The travelled path is obtained from a measurement of the time of arrival of photons from π0 decays. Since neutral kaons from φ-mesons decaying at rest travel with a velocity β∼1/5, time measurements with a precision of allow to determine the flight path of a KL decaying in nπ0 to , for a single detected photon. Such an accuracy is satisfactory for the KLOE aims, provided that the time scale is maintained to an accuracy of a few ps over the entire time of data collection which is of the order of 2–4 years.

The energy scale requirements are not particularly stringent, ∼5% at the maximum energy of . Again the energy calibration must be maintained to much higher accuracy, in average, over long times. This can be achieved with the KLOE detector by a continuous calibration of the relevant scales, simultaneous with data taking, utilizing a host of other processes, from Bhabha scattering events to many φ and kaon decay channels as stated in the original KLOE proposal, see [1] and [2]. Time and energy measurements, combined with momentum measurements with the drift chamber, also provide powerful means for charged particle identification.

This paper describes the KLOE electromagnetic calorimeter and is divided into two parts. The first part, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7, describes the calorimeter structure, component selection, construction techniques, installation and electronics. 8, 9, 10, 11, 12 and 13 deal with energy and time measurements, in particular the calibration procedures and the performance obtained with data from φ-decays.

2. Choices

An ideal electromagnetic calorimeter should provide the best resolution for measuring the energy and the entry point of photons. Special KLOE requirements are very good time resolution, full solid angle coverage and the smallest amount of dead material in front of the calorimeter. Finally, the energy range of interest is . The above considerations have led to the choice of a lead-scintillating fiber sampling calorimeter. Scintillating fiber offers several advantages, in particular they provide good light transmission over the required distances, up to . Superior timing accuracy is obtained because of single mode propagation. Finally, it is easy to adapt the calorimeter shape to geometrical requirements, obtaining good hermeticity. No walls cover the active part of the calorimeter.
